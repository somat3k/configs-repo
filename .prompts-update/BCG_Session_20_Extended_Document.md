- architecture preservation rules

## 5. Go-Live Governance Doctrine

Go-live governance is the permanent operational framework through which the platform decides:
- what changes may enter production
- when they may enter
- under what evidence they may enter
- how risks are reviewed
- who approves critical transitions
- how incidents affect future release permissions
- how new species become part of the biosphere
- how old species are retired without leaving dead architecture behind

Go-live governance must not depend on informal memory or ad hoc authority. It must be explicit, repeatable, role-bound, and auditable.

## 6. Governing Bodies and Authority Classes

## 6.1 Runtime Authority
The Block Controller remains the runtime authority for routeability, lifecycle visibility, and operational admission into live workload lanes.

### Runtime Authority Responsibilities
- recognize live-ready species
- enforce routeability policy
- gate cutover, rollback, and suspension
- reflect degraded and incident state
- preserve controller truth over contested runtime claims

## 6.2 Release Authority
Release authority governs whether a certified species or artifact may proceed into the next environment or live production train.

### Release Authority Responsibilities
- validate gate completion
- validate evidence bundles
- confirm rollback and observability readiness
- ensure compatibility and migration review
- block promotion where unresolved risk exceeds policy

## 6.3 Trust Authority
Trust authority governs security-sensitive approval classes.

### Trust Authority Responsibilities
- approve protected trust-sensitive changes
- validate artifact trust and policy-sensitive species admission
- manage revocations and emergency restrictions
- oversee identity and secret lifecycle policy

## 6.4 Stewardship Authority
Stewardship authority belongs to the owners of each species pack.

### Stewardship Responsibilities
- keep the species pack current
- accept and track operational debt
- maintain runbooks and alerts
- propose changes, migrations, and retirements
- respond to incidents involving the species

## 6.5 Architecture Authority
Architecture authority is responsible for preventing drift across species and transport/runtime law.

### Architecture Responsibilities
- review new species admission
- review protected contract changes
- review mutation classes with system-wide consequences
- preserve the controller/tensor/species constitution

No one authority should silently absorb the responsibilities of all the others.

## 7. Post-Launch Operating Model

After go-live, the platform enters continuous governed operation.

The operating model must support:
- steady production workload
- ongoing session-based improvements
- bounded release trains
- incident response
- emergency containment
- regular audit
- admission of new species
- retirement or merge of obsolete species
- sustained documentation updates

The platform remains a living system, but not an uncontrolled one.

## 8. Release Train Doctrine

## 8.1 Why Release Trains Exist

Release trains prevent production from becoming a chaos stream of unbounded changes. They create predictable windows for:
- admission
- stabilization
- review
- rollback safety
- communication to stewards and operators

## 8.2 Release Cadence Classes

### Regular Release Train
Used for normal production improvements, non-breaking upgrades, certified artifact promotions, and species enhancements.

### Fast-Track Release
Used for low-risk, urgent but bounded changes that have already passed core gates.

### Emergency Release
Used for incidents, trust threats, severe regressions, or critical routeability failures. Requires heightened audit and post-incident review.

### Deferred Release
Used when evidence exists but operational timing, risk bundling, or unresolved dependencies make promotion undesirable.

## 8.3 Release Train Rules
- every release must identify affected species
- every release must carry evidence bundle references
- every release must carry rollback relation
- every release must identify protected contracts or trust-sensitive surfaces
- every release must identify whether it changes routeability, trust posture, or live session semantics
- no production release may bypass documentation and runbook obligations

## 9. Change Classification Doctrine

Every post-launch change must be classified before admission.

### Change Classes
- documentation-only
- observability-only
- configuration-only
- performance tuning
- species-internal logic
- transport or contract change
- tensor contract change
- artifact activation
- trust-sensitive change
- live session or shell behavior change
- species admission
- species retirement
- breaking migration

The change class determines:
- which gates apply
- which authorities approve
- which environments must be crossed
- whether live cutover is allowed
- whether maintenance window is required

## 10. Species Admission Handbook

A new species may join the post-launch ecosystem only if it satisfies the admission standard defined by earlier sessions and passes go-live governance review.

## 10.1 Required Admission Materials
A candidate species must provide:
- full or staged species pack
- declared mission and boundaries
- controller relationship
- transport and tensor obligations
- trust class
- storage ownership
- observability baseline
- certification level
- release evidence
- steward assignment

## 10.2 Admission Stages
- proposed
- incubating
- development-admitted
- staging-admitted
- production-admitted
- protected production-admitted if higher review needed

## 10.3 Admission Rules
- no anonymous or ownerless species
- no species without controller or runtime law alignment
- no species with undocumented side-channel dependencies
- no species may enter production without visible routeability and observability model
- new species must not weaken the fabric’s controller, trust, or tensor constitution

## 11. Species Retirement and Merge Doctrine

Post-launch governance must also define how species leave the ecosystem.

## 11.1 Retirement Conditions
A species may be retired when:
- it is obsolete
- its responsibility has been replaced
- risk exceeds value
- cost of maintenance is unjustified
- architecture simplification requires consolidation

## 11.2 Retirement Rules
- retirement must be planned, not silent
- dependencies must be identified
- contract deprecation path must be explicit
- archival and evidence retention must be preserved
- operational dashboards and runbooks must be updated
- rollback relation must be considered if the retirement itself is reversible

## 11.3 Merge Rules
If two species merge:
- the new ownership model must be explicit
- all moved contracts must be documented
- routeability and trust changes must be reviewed
- the historical species packs must preserve migration notes

## 12. Continuous Improvement Doctrine

Continuous evolution is not casual change. It is governed improvement.

The platform must maintain loops for:
- performance improvement
- incident learning
- contract simplification
- operator-plane refinement
- species pack completeness
- trust hardening
- storage and transport tuning
- model and artifact quality improvement
- tensor and transformation discipline refinement

## 12.1 Improvement Sources
Improvements may originate from:
- hardening results
- incident retrospectives
- operator friction
- performance regressions
- trust or audit findings
- session progression notes
- backlog grooming
- roadmap milestones
- species stewardship proposals

## 12.2 Improvement Rules
- improvements must map to documented change classes
- improvements affecting constitutional surfaces require broader review
- improvements must not silently create shadow patterns outside the doctrine
- improvements should prefer convergence over fragmentation

## 13. Backlog and Roadmap Governance

The backlog after go-live must not become a graveyard or an uncontrolled wish list.

### Backlog Rules
- every backlog item must identify affected species or constitutional surfaces
- every significant item should identify risk class and expected governance path
- undocumented architecture debt must be visible
- urgent work must not permanently crowd out structural work
- trust, hardening, documentation, and observability debt must be tracked explicitly, not hidden under feature work

### Roadmap Rules
- roadmap items must distinguish exploration from commitment
- roadmap items affecting protected contracts or controller law require architecture review
- roadmap items for new species must include admission readiness criteria
- roadmap evolution must preserve the platform’s long-range tensor/species/runtime direction

## 14. Audit and Review Cadence

## 14.1 Cadence Types

### Operational Review
Regular review of health, incident load, alert quality, and routeability trends.

### Release Review
Review of completed releases, regressions, and rollback occurrences.

### Architecture Review
Review of contract drift, transport sprawl, mutation risk, and new species proposals.

### Trust Review
Review of identities, sessions, artifact trust status, denials, and revocations.

### Species Pack Review
Review of documentation drift, ownership clarity, and operational completeness.

### Hardening Review
Review of capacity evidence, unresolved bottlenecks, and retest priorities.

## 14.2 Review Rules
- cadence must be declared, not improvised
- review outcomes must create tracked actions where needed
- recurring failures without governance response are unacceptable
- audit and review outputs must feed backlog and release policy

## 15. Post-Launch Documentation Obligations

Living documents do not stop living after launch.

Every species and constitutional surface must keep:
- session ledger or change ledger
- release notes
- migration notes
- operational runbooks
- roadmap updates
- trust and artifact notes where relevant
- retirement or deprecation notes where relevant

A launch without documentation continuity is the beginning of drift.

## 16. Operational Debt Doctrine

Operational debt is any known weakness that increases runtime, trust, release, or maintenance risk.

Examples:
- incomplete pack coverage
- stale dashboards
- weak replay evidence
- low-confidence rollback
- performance budget gaps
- hidden dependency on manual intervention
- growing incident noise
- undocumented trust exceptions

### Debt Rules
- debt must be visible
- debt must have owner
- debt should carry severity and review horizon
- debt that affects constitutional surfaces must be escalated, not buried

## 17. Incident Learning Loop

Incidents must change the platform.

### Required Incident Outputs
- timeline
- scope
- root-cause candidates
- what governance held
- what governance failed
- what evidence was missing
- what rollback or recovery did
- what preventive actions will be added
- which session or species packs must be updated

No serious incident should end without doctrine feedback.

## 18. Final Governance Matrix

Post-launch governance must always be able to answer:
- who owns a change
- who approved it
- what evidence supported it
- what routeability changed
- what risk remained
- how rollback works
- which documents were updated
- which species were affected
- what audit trail exists
- what future review is scheduled

## 19. Failure Model

## 19.1 Drift Failure
If the system evolves after go-live without alignment to controller, trust, tensor, and species law, governance has failed.

## 19.2 Release Saturation Failure
If too many changes enter without bounded cadence or review, the release system has failed.

## 19.3 Ownerless Evolution Failure
If species mutate without stewardship and document upkeep, continuous evolution has failed.

## 19.4 Governance Bypass Failure
If emergency or convenience paths become the normal release path, the constitution has been bypassed.

## 19.5 Static Platform Failure
If governance becomes so rigid that necessary improvement becomes impossible, the platform has failed in the opposite direction. Continuous evolution must remain possible, but disciplined.
